# Context
- The CTV building was designed and constructed in the mid-1980s as a six-storey office building in Christchurch, New Zealand, using a shear wall protected gravity load system with an eccentric design (shear wall on the north side outside the floor plan).
  - Original: "His design, based on the client’s wishes and Wilkie’s drawings, was a shear wall protected gravity load system. This meant that in an earthquake the lateral forces from the shaking would be absorbed by a shear wall, or walls - the strongest part of the building - while the columns that held up the structure were designed to do pretty much just that. ... The shear wall was on the north side, outside the floor plan altogether, along with the lift shaft and stairs, just as Brooks had suggested." (From document #6, "A special report" section under "THE DESIGN").
- The building was built on alluvial gravel plains with poor drainage and high groundwater, making it prone to liquefaction, but early settlers overlooked these risks when selecting the site.
  - Original: "The site had previously been rejected by New Zealand Company agents (who went on to establish the Nelson settlement) and the Scottish Free Church (who founded Dunedin). They noted, as did Captain Thomas when he originally chose the Lyttelton site, the extensive swamps, the lack of timber, and the difficult access between the only suitable port site on Banks Peninsula and the plains." (From document #7, "Report of the Royal Commission of Investigation" under "Section 2: Christchurch, the City" subsection "2.3.2 The land Christchurch is built on").
- On February 22, 2011, a 6.3 magnitude aftershock caused the CTV building to collapse rapidly (within 20 seconds), killing 115 people (mostly on levels 4-6) and injuring others; this was two-thirds of the earthquake's total death toll.
  - Original: "The collapse had taken no more than 20 seconds. ... The CTV building collapsed during the earthquake that struck Christchurch at 12:51pm on 22 February 2011. The collapse resulted in the death of 115 people and others suffered serious injuries." (From document #6, "A special report" under "Stages of collapse" and from document #7, "Report of the Royal Commission of Investigation" under "Volume 6: CTV building").
- Post-collapse, fires broke out, complicating rescues; 19 people on the top floor survived, but lower floors pancaked, trapping and killing others, including 71 students and 9 teachers from King's Education on level 4.
  - Original: "There had been 19 people on the top floor of the CTV building who rode it down to the ground. All but one of them made it out alive. One hundred and fourteen other people in the building did not. ... Seventy-one students and nine teachers from King’s died when the building collapsed three years later - a far higher casualty rate than if level 4 had remained an office." (From document #6, "A special report" under "Stages of collapse" and "In 2008, King’s Education, a school for English as a second language, moved into level 4 of the building.").
- The collapse triggered a Royal Commission inquiry, police investigation, and engineering disciplinary actions; no prosecutions occurred by 2017, despite calls for corporate manslaughter charges.
  - Original: "On November 30, police told the families of victims they would not prosecute anyone in relation to the CTV building collapse. ... Police finished their report earlier this year. Read said an updated version was delivered to the Christchurch Crown solicitors last week. The next step was Crown Law." (From document #6, "A special report" under "This story was first published on June 3, 2017." and "The CTV collapse, and the Pike River mine disaster... sparked renewed calls for a corporate manslaughter charge.").

# Causes
- Design deficiencies: The building's eccentric shear wall design led to excessive inter-storey deflections; ETABS analysis was flawed (e.g., no corner deflection calculations), and a second south shear wall was added but inadequately coupled, failing code requirements.
  - Original: "The first time Harding ran the numbers, ETABS predicted deflections greater than what was allowed under New Zealand building standards. ... Harding didn’t do any [corner deflection calculations], which meant he had numbers telling him the floors in the building would move less in an earthquake than they actually would. ... The building shouldn’t have got a consent, but it did." (From document #6, "A special report" under "THE DESIGN" and "The application was lodged on July 17, 1986.").
- Construction flaws: Reinforcing bars in beams were bent back instead of embedded in the north shear wall; concrete surfaces at beam-column joints were not roughened; columns lacked adequate confinement for ductility.
  - Original: "The concrete surfaces where the columns and beams met should have been roughened, but weren’t. ... Instead of being embedded in the wall, the reinforcing bars inside in the beams were bent back onto themselves, coming to rest hard up against the wall. ... The CTV building should never have been built. Parts of its columns, beam-column joints and the floor-wall connections did not meet building standards." (From document #6, "A special report" under "Construction on the CTV building began in late 1986" and "Key causes of the collapse").
- Permitting errors: Christchurch City Council approved the building permit despite identified non-compliance (e.g., floor-shear wall connections); pressure from engineer Alan Reay influenced approval without fixes.
  - Original: "Tapper wasn’t happy. He broke the habit of a lifetime and told wife Pat of his concerns about the building’s earthquake risk and the “huge pressure” from Bluck to sign off on the design. ... The building shouldn’t have got a consent, but it did." (From document #6, "A special report" under "The application was lodged on July 17, 1986." and "Whatever was discussed in Bluck’s conversation with Reay, it wasn’t enough.").
- Post-September 2010 inspections inadequate: Cracks were noted but no structural drawings reviewed; green placards issued despite concerns, missing critical weaknesses like floor-wall separation.
  - Original: "His report concluded there was no evidence of structural failure. The findings may have been different if he had been given the structural and architectural drawings of the building that he asked to see. ... Despite this, they filled out an L2 assessment form, issuing another green placard." (From document #6, "A special report" under "On September 4, 2010" and "Two days later the building was identified as one of three in need of an immediate, more rigorous re-inspection.").
- Collapse sequence: Drag bars failed, disconnecting floors from north shear wall; columns lost axial load capacity due to excessive deflection, leading to pancaking.
  - Original: "First, the drag bars connecting the floors to the north shear wall failed, potentially causing the floors to disconnect from the wall. Then, the inter-storey deflection was too much for the columns to withstand, triggering a loss of “axial load carrying capacity”, meaning the floors started to collapse." (From document #7, "Report of the Royal Commission of Investigation" under "Volume 6: CTV building" and from document #6, "A special report" under "VIDEO: JOHN KIRK-ANDERSON/FAIRFAX NZ").
- Engineer inexperience and oversight failure: David Harding lacked multi-storey experience; Alan Reay provided no supervision, assuming competence; both implicated in non-compliance.
  - Original: "We have concluded that a primary reason for [the non-compliance] was that Mr Harding was working beyond his competence in designing this building. ... Dr Reay should not have left Mr Harding to work unsupervised on the design or without a system in place for reviewing the design, either by himself or someone else qualified to do so." (From document #7, "Report of the Royal Commission of Investigation" under "Volume 6: CTV building" and from document #6, "A special report" under "Reay was pensive, deliberate on the stand...").

# Ethical Implications for Engineers
- Misrepresentation of competence: Harding undertook multi-storey design without experience, signing off without review; breached IPENZ Code of Ethics (1986) by misrepresenting skills.
  - Original: "The Disciplinary Committee considered that Mr Harding met the ethical requirement to disclose the limits of his competence. However, by signing and submitting the design documentation for the CTV building to the Christchurch City Council, Mr Harding effectively misrepresented his competence." (From document #2, "Report from Engineering New Zealand’s Disciplinary Committee").
  - Applicable Code: From document #4, "Engineering New Zealand’s Code of Ethics" under "4. Act competently": "You must... only undertake engineering activities that are within your competence; and... must not misrepresent, or permit others to misrepresent, your competence."
- Failure to report adverse consequences: Engineers (e.g., Hare in 1990) identified non-compliant floor-wall connections but remedial work was incomplete; no escalation to authorities despite risks.
  - Original: "Hare visited the ARCE offices, now trading as Alan Reay Consultants Ltd (ARCL), to tell them about the problem and the need for potentially expensive remedial work. ... Finally in October, steel drag bars were fitted on levels 4, 5, and 6... ARCL should have applied for a permit for their installation, but didn’t." (From document #6, "A special report" under "In 2001, when the Going Places language school planned to move into the building").
  - Applicable Code: From document #4, "Engineering New Zealand’s Code of Ethics" under "3. Report adverse consequences": "If you have reasonable grounds to believe that an engineering matter has, or could have, adverse consequences you must bring the matter to the notice of the relevant regulatory body..."
- Lack of oversight and review: Reay failed to supervise Harding, leading to deficient design; breached ethical duty for proper review in small firms.
  - Original: "Dr Reay should have known better, the commissioners said: '[We] ... consider that [he] was aware of Mr Harding’s lack of relevant experience and therefore should have realised that this design was pushing him beyond the limits of his competence.'" (From document #6, "A special report" under "The commission unearthed myriad failures...").
  - Applicable Code: From document #4, "Engineering New Zealand’s Code of Ethics" under "4. Act competently": "You... must not knowingly permit other engineers for whose engineering activities you are responsible to: undertake engineering activities that are outside their competence; undertake engineering activities in a manner that is not careful and competent..."
- Confidentiality vs. public safety: Engineers faced conflicts in reporting weaknesses (e.g., 1990 Holmes report); code requires disclosure if harm is likely, but process protects reviewers.
  - Original: "Reviewing structural engineers should have a clearly expressed ethical duty to disclose the existence of a critical structural weakness, in a process which protects them from any liability where they have acted in good faith." (From document #7, "Report of the Royal Commission of Investigation" under "Section 4: Training and education of civil engineers and organisation of the civil engineering profession").
  - Applicable Code: From document #4, "Engineering New Zealand’s Code of Ethics" under "7. Maintain confidentiality": "You may disclose confidential information if, and to the extent that you are required to disclose the information in order to comply with rule 3 or rule 8 [report adverse consequences or breaches]..."
- Breach of ethical conduct in resignation: Harding and Reay resigned from IPENZ before disciplinary hearings, seen as lacking integrity; led to rule changes to prevent evasion.
  - Original: "The resignations from Ipenz of Harding and his former boss, Alan Reay, were 'disappointing to say the least'. The move could be viewed as 'a lack of integrity and ethical behaviour on behalf of a professional engineer', Elms said." (From document #5, "An article focusing on one of the engineers involved in the collapse").
  - Applicable Code: From document #4, "Engineering New Zealand’s Code of Ethics" under "8. Report breach of Code": "If you have reasonable grounds to believe that another Member has committed a significant breach of the Code of Ethical Conduct you must report the matter to Engineering New Zealand." (Implies ongoing accountability; post-incident, IPENZ updated rules to continue investigations after resignation).
