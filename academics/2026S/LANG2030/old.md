## The Four "Ds"
- ### Describe 
  - the stakeholders
  - the fact of the case
  - causes of problems
  - what harm has been done, to be caused
- ### Determine
  - ethical issues from the case
  - how can it be defined
  - What is wrong 
  - What ethical code have b een breached
- ### Discuss 
  - options for the stakeholders
- ### Decide
  - evaluate each options
  - which options is the most ethical

## Our 
There are three main oversights that led to the Deepwater Horizon Disaster. The first was the technical oversight of the operation. Because the disaster was technically caused by the undetected entry of hydrocarbons into the oil well, the failure to adhere to and maintain up-to-date regulatory safety requirements allowed the particles to exploit the weaknesses in the system’s defenses and develop a blowout, violating WFEO’s principle that engineers must “practice in accordance with accepted engineering practices, standards, and codes”. However, such technical causes were deeply rooted in the oversight of both the regulatory systems and the poor safety culture within the company. BP’s culture favored cutting costs & financial incentives, but not safety, and thus sophisticated and costly regulations to deal with increased safety and environmental risks of deepwater drilling were not implemented. Such a lack of regulations and the lack of safety equipment meant the high-pressure gas emission could not be recognized immediately after it took place, causing multiple explosions and eventually eleven deaths. In conclusion, technical, regulatory, and organizational failures all contributed to the fatal accident, where the lack of safety culture within the organization is the root cause. Poor organizational culture led to weak regulatory systems, and in turn, the lack of safety equipment, as well as accidents.
