- ### Key concepts in statistics:
  - Population
    - collection of all possible objects or individual of interests 
      - too large, can't deal with
  - Sample:
    - part of the population selected for analysis
      - Smaller, can deal with
  - Parameter:
    - a numerical measures computed from the population and used to describe a characteristics of the population

  - Statistic
    - a numerical measures computed from the sample and used to describe a characteristics of the sample
      - can compute

  - Population
    - tossing the coin to get a head of tail
  - Sample:
    - results over just 10 tosses
  - Parameter:
    - probability of head
  - Statistics:
    - Compute from the sample

  - Population
    - a collection of all families in Hong Kong
  - Sample:
    - 1000 families randomly selected
  - Parameter:
    - the average monthly family spending on food in Hong Kong
  - Statistic:
    - the average monthly family spending on food of the 1000 families selected

  - impossible or impractical to examine the entire population
  - instead examine a part of it (sampmle)

- ## Independence
  - Note that it is possible that an event $B$ occuring does not influence the probability of $A$ occuring
  - **For example,** a fair coin is tossed twice. Consider the following events: $A, B$: the event that a head occurs on the first/second toss, respectively.
    - intuitively, there is no relationship between the occurrence of $A$ and $B$ 
    - **mathematically**, $A$ and $B$ are independent:

      >$$
      >\iff P(A|B) = P(A), \quad P(B|A) = P(B) \\
      >\iff P(A \cap B) = P(A|B)P(B) = P(B|A)P(A) = P(A)P(B) 
      >$$

    - **Definition**: 
      - if $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$
        - then the events $A$ and $B$ are said to be **independent**
      - if they are **not independent**
        - then they are said to be **dependent**
      - if $P(B) > 0$, then $A, B$ are indepdendent if $P(A|B) = P(A)$
        - in other words, know that $B$ occurs 
          - does not affect the probability of $A$ occuring
    - Proposition:
      - If $A, B$ are independent, then $A$ and $B^c$ are also independent
        - proof:

          >$$
          >\begin{aligned}
          >P(A \cap B^c) &= P(A) - P(A \cap B)  \\
          >&= P(A) - P(A)P(B) \\
          >&= P(A)(1 - P(B)) \\
          >&= P(A)P(B^c)
          >\end{aligned}
          >$$

        - **Remark:**
          - the following statements are equivalent:
            - $A$ and $B$ are independent
            - $A$ and $B^c$ are independent
            - $A^c$ and $B$ are independent
            - $A^c$ and $B^c$ are independent
          - $A$ and $B$ are independent $\iff P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$
          - $A$ and $B$ are disjoint $\iff A \cap B = \emptyset$
  - ### Mutually independent:
    - **definition:** events $A$, $B$ an $C$ are said to be mutually independent if and only if the following are all true:
      >$$
      >\text{ Mutually independent }
      >\begin{cases}
      >\text{ Pairwise independent }
      >\begin{cases}
      >P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B) \\
      >P(B \cap C) = P(B)P(C) \\
      >P(C \cap A) = P(C)P(A) \\
      >\end{cases} \\
      >\quad \quad \quad P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A)P(B)P(C) \\
      >\end{cases}
      >$$

    - **Remark 1:** Pairwise independent may not mean Mutually independent:
      - example:
        - $S = \{s_1, s_2, s_3, s_4\}$ with 4 equally likely outcomes
        - events: $A = \{s_1, s_2\}$, $B = \{s_1, s_3\}$, $C = \{s_1, s_4\}$
          >$$
          >P(A \cap B) = \frac{1}{4} = P(A)P(B) \\
          >P(A \cap C) = \frac{1}{4} = P(A)P(C) \\
          >P(B \cap C) = \frac{1}{4} = P(A)P(C) \\
          >
          >\text{ So they are pairwise independent } \\
          >\quad \\ 
          >\text {However, } P(A \cap B \cap C) = \frac{1}{4} \neq P(A)P(B)P(C) \\
          >\text{ So they are not mutually independent } \\
          >$$
    - **Remark 2:** $\quad P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A)P(B)P(C)$ does not mean pairwise independent
      - Example:
        - Suppose $A = B$ and $C = \emptyset$
        - Although $A \cap B \cap C = \emptyset$, which results in
          - $P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A)P(B)P(C)$
        - the events $A$ and $B$ are not independent
- ## Law of total probability
  - motivation: how to calculate the probability of event $A$ in some complicated scenario
  - by the venn diagram, $A \cap B$ and $A \cap B^c$ are disjoint
  - Therefore, we can write event $A$ as:
    - $A = (A \cap B) \cup (A \cap B^c)$
    - so:
    - $P(A) = P(A \cap B) + P(A \cap B^c)$
    - then have the result often known as "law of total probability":
    - for $0 < P(B) < 1$:
      - $P(A) = P(A|B)P(B) + P(A|B^c)P(B^c)$
  - Note that $B$ and $B^c$ forms a partition of the sample space $\Omega$ in the formula:
    - $P(A) = P(A|B)P(B) + P(A|B^c)P(B^c)$
    - as:
      - $B \cup B^c = \Omega$ and $B \cap B^c = \emptyset$
  - Events $B_1, B_2, \dots, B_k$ is a called a **partition** if:
    - of the sample space $\Omega$:
      - they are mutually exclusive:
        - $(B_i \cap B_j = \emptyset, \quad i \neq j, \quad i, j = 1, \dots, k )$ 
      - they are exhaustive:
        - $(B_1, B_2, \dots, B_k = \Omega)$
      - ![alt text](attachments/image-13.png)
      - <u>Law of total probability:</u>
        - for partition $B_1, B_2, \dots, B_k$:
          - $P(A) = P(B_1)P(A|B_1) + P(B_2)P(A|B_2) + \dots + P(B_k)P(A|B_k)$
- ## Bayes theorem:
  - motivation: relationship between $P(B|A)$ and $P(A|B)$ 
    - e.g. find $P(B|A)$ given $P(A|B)$
  - **Theorem:** 
    - if $P(A) > 0, \quad P(B) > 0$:
      >$$
      >P(B|A) = P(A|B)\frac{P(B)}{P(A)} = \frac{P(A|B)P(B)}{P(A|B)P(B) + P(A|B^c)P(B^c)}
      >$$

  - $P(B)$ is called the {{prior probability}}
  - $P(B|A)$ is called the {{posterior probability}}
  - ### Generalized version:
  - **Theorem:** 
    - if $B_1, B_2, \dots, B_k$ is a partition and $P(B_i) > 0$

      >$$
      >P(B_i|A) = \frac{P(A|B_i)P(B_i)}{\sum_{j = 1}^{k}{P(A|B_j)P(B_j)}}
      >$$
